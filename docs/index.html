Procedural railway video, engineered for defect-detection AI.

TSV Twin is an open digital twin of railway track. It builds the track section by section — grounded in real rail-profile standards — and flies a camera over it to render synthetic video with a controllable catalogue of geometric defects. Because the scene is generated, ground truth is known by construction: perfectly labelled training data for the failure modes real datasets never capture, without a single field recording.

The problem

Real inspection footage is scarce, skewed, and locked away

Automated railway defect detection lives or dies by its training data — and that data barely exists. Public imagery is heavily skewed toward rail-surface faults like squats, shellings and cracks, because they are visible and common. The failure modes that matter most — track-geometry deviations such as lateral displacement and differential settlement — are underrepresented, and to our knowledge uncovered by any public dataset.

The reasons compound. In well-maintained networks, monitoring systems — vibration analysis, eddy-current and ultrasonic testing — catch defects long before they are visible to a camera, so there is little to photograph. Operators guard their inspection data as operationally sensitive. And the simulation tools that could close the gap are proprietary and closed.

TSV Twin's answer: generate the footage instead. Build the track procedurally, deform it in physically-motivated ways, and render it — so the rare, safety-critical cases become available on demand, perfectly labelled.
The approach

An open digital twin, generated from open data

TSV Twin builds railway track procedurally in Blender and deforms it in physically-motivated ways, so the position, type and severity of every defect is chosen by the pipeline and therefore known exactly. The result is a scalable, transparent source of the balanced, fully-labelled data that safety-critical detection systems need — and that the real world refuses to hand over.

Parametric track geometry

Track is assembled from cached H-shaped sections — rails, sleepers, fasteners and clips — validated against real rail-profile standards (UIC54, UIC60, AREMA 115RE). Change the config, get a different, dimensionally-faithful stretch of track.

Geometry defects on demand

A registry of 13 physically-motivated deformations — gauge widening and narrowing, lateral and inward shift, vertical bumps, missing fasteners — the operationally serious, data-starved category. Injected probabilistically or forced.

Ground truth for free

Because the scene is generated, every defect's location and class is known by construction — no manual labelling, no ambiguity, and rare cases on tap.

Repeatable presets

From 640×360 drafts to 4K production runs — one command each. Deterministic output makes a growing, balanced dataset easy to assemble over time.

Physically-based materials

Rails, sleepers, ballast and fasteners carry procedural PBR shading — metallic response, roughness, surface detail — so light behaves as it does on oxidised steel and weathered concrete, not a flat photo.

Cached & headless

Section prototypes are built once and reused, with automatic source-fingerprint versioning — edit the geometry and stale assets rebuild themselves. Runs headless in Blender's bpy, with an ffmpeg fallback to MP4.

See it in motion

The same procedurally generated track, filmed from four camera rigs the pipeline ships with. These are short draft renders — placeholders until higher-resolution sequences land — but the geometry, materials and defects are the real output.

Defect catalogue

Thirteen defect types, all ground-truth labelled

Track-geometry deviations are the category public datasets miss — and the one this project targets first. Each defect is a physically-motivated deformation: rail-displacement types bend the rail along a smooth half-sine arch across several sections, so the bend is continuous and realistic rather than a single kinked vertex.

Grounded

Built on open standards and open data

None of this is invented from thin air. The geometry follows published rail standards, and the realism draws on open-access railway datasets — the same sources that let synthetic footage stand in for the real thing.

UIC54 · UIC60 · AREMA 115RE

Rail cross-sections and sleeper dimensions are validated against real profile standards (UIC leaflet 860; AREMA Manual for Railway Engineering, Ch. 4). Feed in a geometry config and any out-of-spec dimension is flagged and corrected.

RailSem19

8,500 annotated sequences shot from the cab of trains and trams across 38 countries, four seasons and varied weather — the reference for intact-component textures and background terrain under real lighting.

Rail-surface defect sets

~6,800 high-resolution images across surface-fault classes — cracks, shellings, squats, spallings, seams and more — supplying material-level defect signatures for weathering and wear.

Railway Track Surface Faults (MUET)

5,200+ close-range railhead images at 120 fps across seven fault classes, captured with a focused 14-inch field of view — fine-grained ground truth for material degradation.

How it works

One pass, from empty scene to annotated MP4

The whole run is a single execution path orchestrated by the render pipeline.

Prepare the scene

Clean the Blender scene, set up the world, lighting, and render settings from the chosen preset.

Build the track

Instantiate cached healthy and defective section prototypes and chain them into a continuous run of track.

Inject defects

The defect selector probabilistically starts defects (~10% of sections); multi-section spans queue their own follower positions for continuity.

Fly the camera

Animate a camera over the track to produce the flyover motion at the configured resolution, duration, and frame rate.

Render & assemble

Render frames and write the MP4 — falling back to a PNG sequence assembled with ffmpeg when the Blender build lacks a video codec.

The bigger picture

Part of Track Sentry Vision

TSV Twin is the rendering half of Track Sentry Vision (TSV) — a Walloon Region-funded effort by UCLouvain, UMons and industry partners to build a low-cost, autonomous preventative-maintenance system for railways in developing regions. It was TSV's own detection pipeline that ran into the data bottleneck this project sets out to fix.

① Parametric track & defects shipping

This repository: physically-motivated 3D track geometry, a growing catalogue of geometric defects, and a render pipeline that turns them into video whose ground truth is known by construction.

② Texture extraction from real imagery in progress

A pipeline that segments real photographs of track components with vision foundation models (e.g. SAM) and derives tileable PBR maps — albedo, roughness, surface normals — so synthetic surfaces match field-deployed infrastructure. Released as a standalone, reusable open-source library.

Get started

Render your first clip

You'll need Blender 5.1+ and ffmpeg. TSV Twin runs inside Blender's Python interpreter — never as standalone python.

# 1. Clone and install dependencies (uv)
git clone https://github.com/ikostis-ucl/rail_defect_sim.git
cd rail_defect_sim
uv sync

# 2. Render a quick draft (640×360, ~2s)
./runtime/draft_quick.sh

# 3. Or a full preview / production run
./runtime/draft_preview.sh
./runtime/final_fullhd.sh
# The canonical invocation, spelled out:
blender --background --python run_video_gen.py

# Pass overrides after `--`
blender --background --python run_video_gen.py -- \
    --duration-seconds 10 \
    --resolution-x 1920 --resolution-y 1080

# Output lands in data/output/<run_name>/
Full installation and configuration notes — including portable, root-free installs of Blender and ffmpeg — live in the project README.
